How this CD calculator works

A certificate of deposit grows by compound interest: the bank pays interest on your deposit, then pays interest on that interest each compounding period. The maturity value is found with the standard formula:

FV = P · (1 + r / m)m · t

where P is your deposit, r is the annual interest rate (as a decimal), m is the number of times interest compounds per year (12 for monthly, 365 for daily, 4 for quarterly, 1 for annually), and t is the term in years — your term in months divided by 12. Interest earned is just the maturity value minus your original deposit.

APY vs APR — what your bank is really quoting

This trips a lot of savers up, so it is worth being precise:

  • APR (annual percentage rate) is the nominal rate before compounding. A 4.5% APR compounded monthly does not actually return 4.5% over the year — it returns a touch more.
  • APY (annual percentage yield) is what you actually earn after compounding is baked in. Banks are required to advertise CDs by APY, so the headline number on a CD offer is almost always the APY.

The Effective APY field above shows the true annual yield produced by the rate and compounding frequency you entered. Two practical tips: if your bank only gave you an APY, set compounding to Annually — an APY already includes compounding, so compounding it again would overstate your return. If your bank gave you a nominal APR plus a compounding schedule, leave the frequency on the schedule they quoted and the Effective APY will tell you the real yield.

How to get the most out of a CD

  • Compare by APY, not APR. A higher APY always means more money in your pocket for the same deposit and term — it is the one apples-to-apples number.
  • Match the term to your timeline. Early withdrawal usually triggers a penalty, so only lock money you will not need before maturity.
  • Consider a CD ladder. Splitting your cash across several terms keeps part of it maturing regularly, so you stay liquid while still capturing longer-term rates.
  • Check what happens at maturity. Many CDs auto-renew into a new term at the prevailing rate unless you tell the bank otherwise within the grace period.

Frequently asked questions

What is a certificate of deposit (CD)?

A CD is a savings product where you lock a fixed deposit with a bank for a set term — often 3 months to 5 years — in exchange for a fixed interest rate that's usually higher than a regular savings account. You get your deposit back plus interest at maturity.

What's the difference between APY and APR on a CD?

APR is the stated nominal annual rate before compounding. APY (annual percentage yield) is what you actually earn once interest compounds during the year, so it is a little higher. Banks usually advertise CDs by APY. If your bank quoted only an APY, set compounding to Annually so the calculator does not compound it twice.

How is my CD's maturity value calculated?

We use the compound-interest formula FV = P(1 + r/m)m·t, where P is your deposit, r is the annual rate, m is the number of compounding periods per year, and t is the term in years. Interest earned is simply the maturity value minus your original deposit.

Can I withdraw money from a CD early?

Usually yes, but most banks charge an early-withdrawal penalty — often a few months of interest — which can eat into or even exceed what you've earned. This calculator shows the full-term value and does not subtract any early-withdrawal penalty.
