How this personal loan calculator works

A personal loan is a fixed-rate, fully-amortizing loan: you borrow a lump sum and repay it in equal monthly installments. The payment is found with the standard amortizing-loan formula:

M = P · r(1 + r)n / ((1 + r)n − 1)

where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate (your APR ÷ 12), and n is the number of monthly payments (term in years × 12). Early on, most of each payment goes to interest; as the balance falls, more goes to principal. The yearly schedule above shows exactly how that split shifts over the life of the loan.

What changes your monthly payment

Three inputs drive everything, and they pull in different directions:

  • Loan amount. Borrow more and both the payment and the total interest rise in step.
  • APR. The interest rate is fixed for the whole term, so even a couple of points changes the total cost meaningfully — shop the APR, not the monthly payment.
  • Term. A longer term lowers the monthly payment but you make more payments, so you pay more interest overall. A shorter term costs more per month but far less in total.

How extra payments save you money

Most personal loans carry no prepayment penalty, so every extra dollar you add goes straight to the principal. A smaller balance accrues less interest the very next month, which compounds into real savings. Frequently asked questions

How is a personal loan monthly payment calculated?

Personal loans are fixed, fully-amortizing loans, so the payment uses M = P · r(1+r)n / ((1+r)n − 1). Each payment covers that month's interest first, and the remainder reduces your balance.

What is a good interest rate (APR) for a personal loan?

Rates typically run from about 7% for excellent credit to 30%+ for lower scores, and are fixed for the life of the loan. A lower APR or shorter term both cut total interest — compare the APR, not just the monthly payment, since a longer term can hide a higher total cost.

Do extra payments pay off a personal loan faster?

Yes. With no prepayment penalty, any extra amount goes straight to principal, shrinking the balance faster and cutting both the number of payments and the total interest. Enter an extra amount above to see the interest and time you save.

Does this calculator include origination fees?

No — it works from the loan amount you enter. Many lenders deduct a 1%–8% origination fee from the funds you receive while still charging interest on the full amount, so your real APR can be higher. Enter the full loan amount the lender charges interest on.
