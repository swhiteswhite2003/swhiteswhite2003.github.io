How this auto loan calculator works

First we find the amount financed — the part of the car you actually borrow:

Amount financed = vehicle price − down payment − trade-in value

Your monthly payment then comes from the standard amortizing-loan formula:

M = P · r(1 + r)n / ((1 + r)n − 1)

where P is the amount financed, r is the monthly interest rate (your APR ÷ 12), and n is the number of monthly payments (the loan term — 36, 48, 60, 72, or 84 months). Every payment is split between interest on the remaining balance and principal that pays the loan down; early payments are mostly interest, later ones mostly principal.

Why the term you pick matters more than the monthly payment

Stretching a car loan to 72 or 84 months makes the monthly number look friendlier, but it is one of the most expensive habits in personal finance. You pay interest for years longer, and because a car loses value far faster than the loan balance falls, you can spend most of the term upside-down — owing more than the car is worth. Switch the term above from 60 to 84 months and watch the total interest climb while the loan amount stays the same.

Three ways to cut what your car really costs

  • Put more down. A larger down payment or trade-in lowers the amount financed, so both the monthly payment and the total interest drop — and you avoid going upside-down.
  • Shop the APR, not just the payment. A point or two off your rate can save hundreds. Get pre-approved at a bank or credit union before you set foot in the dealership.
  • Choose the shortest term you can afford. A 48- or 60-month loan costs far less in interest than 72 or 84 months for the same car.

What this estimate does and doesn't include

The result covers principal and interest only on the amount you finance. It does not add sales tax, title, registration, documentation or dealer fees, or optional add-ons like gap insurance and extended warranties. If those will be rolled into your loan, include them in the vehicle price so the payment reflects the real total. It also excludes ongoing costs of ownership — fuel, insurance, and maintenance — which you should budget separately.

Frequently asked questions

How is my monthly car payment calculated?

It uses M = P · r(1+r)n / ((1+r)n − 1), where P is the amount financed (price minus down payment and trade-in), r is your APR ÷ 12, and n is the term in months. A higher APR or longer term changes both the payment and the total interest.

How do a down payment and trade-in affect my loan?

Both cut the amount you finance dollar-for-dollar. The loan equals price minus down payment minus trade-in, so a bigger down payment or trade-in lowers your monthly payment, reduces total interest, and helps you avoid owing more than the car is worth.

Is a 72- or 84-month car loan a good idea?

It lowers the monthly payment but you pay much more interest overall, and you can stay upside-down for years because cars depreciate quickly. Compare a 60-month term against 72 or 84 months above and watch the total interest jump before committing.

Does this include tax, title, and fees?

No — it estimates principal and interest on the amount financed. Sales tax, title, registration, dealer fees, and add-ons like gap insurance or an extended warranty aren't included unless you add them to the vehicle price you enter.
