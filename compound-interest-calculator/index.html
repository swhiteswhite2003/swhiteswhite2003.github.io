How this compound interest calculator works

The future value of a starting balance plus equal contributions is found with the compound-growth formula:

FV = P(1 + i)N + PMT · ((1 + i)N − 1) / i

where P is your initial investment, PMT is the contribution each period, i is the periodic interest rate (your annual return rate ÷ the number of periods per year), and N is the total number of periods (years × periods per year). With monthly compounding that means 12 periods a year; quarterly uses 4 and annually uses 1. When the rate is 0%, the formula simplifies to your principal plus every contribution, with no interest.

Why compounding is so powerful

Compound interest means you earn returns on your returns. In the early years most of your balance growth comes from the money you put in. But as the interest builds, it begins to earn its own interest — and eventually that compounding can out-earn your contributions entirely. The longer your money stays invested, the more dramatic the effect, which is why starting early usually beats trying to catch up later with bigger deposits.

The biggest levers on your final balance

  • Time. Years in the market matter more than almost anything else — the curve bends upward late, so an extra five or ten years can dwarf a higher contribution.
  • Contribution amount. Steady monthly deposits add fresh principal that then compounds. Use the field above to test what an extra $100 a month does.
  • Return rate. A higher rate compounds faster, but be realistic — chasing returns usually means more risk. Compare a conservative and an optimistic rate side by side.
  • Compounding frequency. More frequent compounding helps a little; its impact grows at higher rates and over longer periods.

How to read your results

The big number is your projected future value — what the account could be worth at the end of the period. Total invested is everything you put in (your initial amount plus all contributions), and total interest earned is the growth on top of that. By design, total invested plus total interest equals the future value. The growth multiple shows how many times your money you end up with, and the yearly table reveals how the interest-per-year quietly overtakes your deposits.

Frequently asked questions

What is compound interest?

Compound interest is interest earned on both your original principal and the interest already added to it. Because the interest itself starts earning interest, your balance grows faster the longer you stay invested — that snowball effect is what makes time the most powerful input.

How does compounding frequency affect my return?

More frequent compounding (monthly versus annually) credits interest to your balance sooner, so that interest also starts earning. The difference is small at low rates but grows with higher rates and longer time horizons. Switch between monthly, quarterly and annual above to compare.

Does this calculator include regular contributions?

Yes. Enter a contribution amount and it is added every period — monthly by default, or once per quarter or per year if you change the compounding frequency. The future value, total contributions and total interest figures all include those ongoing deposits.

What annual return rate should I use?

Use a realistic long-run estimate rather than a single good year. Historically a diversified stock-market portfolio has returned roughly 7% per year after inflation, while cash savings accounts earn far less. Lower the rate for a conservative projection and compare a few scenarios.
