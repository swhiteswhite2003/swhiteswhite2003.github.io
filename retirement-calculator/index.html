How this retirement calculator works

Your projected balance combines two things: your current savings growing on their own, and the stream of monthly contributions you keep adding. Both compound month after month using the future-value formula:

FV = P(1 + i)N + PMT · ((1 + i)N − 1) / i

where P is your current savings, PMT is your monthly contribution, i is the monthly return (your expected annual return ÷ 12), and N is the number of months until retirement (retirement age − current age, × 12). The growth from returns figure is simply your projected nest egg minus everything you actually put in.

The 4% rule: turning a nest egg into income

A balance is only useful if it can pay your bills. The widely-cited 4% rule suggests you can withdraw about 4% of your portfolio in your first year of retirement and adjust for inflation after that, with a high chance of the money lasting roughly 30 years. The "Est. income (4% rule)" box turns your projected nest egg into an approximate monthly paycheck on that basis. Flip it around and you get the savings target: you need roughly 25× your desired annual spending.

Three levers that move your number the most

  • Start earlier. Time is the most powerful input here — contributions made in your 20s have decades to compound. Try lowering your current age and watch the nest egg jump.
  • Contribute more. Even an extra $100 a month, compounded over 30+ years, can add a surprising amount to the final balance.
  • Mind the return. A single percentage point of return, sustained for decades, can change the outcome dramatically. Use a realistic, slightly conservative number rather than your best-case year.

Frequently asked questions

How much money do I need to retire?

A common starting point is the 4% rule: you need roughly 25 times your desired annual spending. If you want $40,000 a year from your portfolio, that points to about a $1,000,000 nest egg. The calculator shows the flip side — an estimated safe monthly income equal to 4% of your projected balance.

Does this include inflation and taxes?

No. The result is a pre-inflation, pre-tax projection in today's dollars. To approximate an inflation-adjusted figure, enter a return that is about 2–3 points lower than your nominal expectation. Withdrawals from a traditional 401(k) or IRA are also generally taxable, which this estimate does not model.

What expected return should I use?

Historically a stock-heavy portfolio has averaged around 7% a year before inflation, while a balanced stock-and-bond mix is often modeled at 5–6%. There are no guarantees, so choose a number you are comfortable with and compare a few scenarios.

Why does starting early matter so much?

Compounding rewards time. Money invested early earns returns, and those returns earn their own returns for decades. That is why a dollar saved in your 20s can be worth several times more at retirement than the same dollar saved later.
