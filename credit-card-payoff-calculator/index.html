How this credit card payoff calculator works

Credit cards charge interest every month on whatever you still owe. Each month the calculator does three things in order:

  1. Adds one month of interest: interest = balance × (APR ÷ 12).
  2. Subtracts your fixed monthly payment.
  3. Carries the new, lower balance into the next month.

It repeats until the balance hits zero, counting the months along the way. That month count becomes your payoff time and debt-free date, and the interest added each month is summed into total interest paid.

The minimum payment trap

The most important number on this page is the interest added in your first month. If your monthly payment is only a little more than that, almost all of it is eaten by interest and your balance barely moves — stretching a payoff into years and many times the interest. If your payment is less than the monthly interest, the balance actually grows and can never be paid off. That's why making only the card's minimum payment can keep you in debt for decades.

The break-even line is simple: your payment has to clear balance × (APR ÷ 12) before a single dollar reduces what you owe. Everything above that line is real progress.

Three ways to get debt-free faster

  • Pay more than the minimum. Every extra dollar skips interest entirely and goes straight to principal. Raise the payment above and watch the months drop.
  • Lower your APR. A balance-transfer card or a personal loan at a lower rate means less of each payment is lost to interest.
  • Stop adding new charges. This calculator assumes no new spending. New purchases reset your progress, so pause the card while you pay it down.

Frequently asked questions

How long will it take to pay off my credit card?

It depends on your balance, your APR, and how much you pay each month. The calculator charges interest on the balance every month, applies your fixed payment, and counts how many months it takes to reach zero — then shows the date and the total interest you'll pay.

Why does my balance barely go down each month?

Each month interest is added first, equal to your balance times your APR divided by 12. Only the part of your payment left over after that interest reduces the balance. If your payment is close to the monthly interest, almost nothing comes off the principal — and if it is below the interest, the balance can never be cleared.

How is credit card interest calculated?

Credit cards charge interest monthly on the outstanding balance. The monthly rate is your APR divided by 12, so a 22.9% APR is about 1.91% per month. On a $6,000 balance that's roughly $114 of interest in the first month alone.

Will paying a little more each month really help?

Yes — dramatically. Because interest compounds on whatever is left, every extra dollar you pay goes straight to principal and stops accruing interest. Raising your monthly payment even modestly can cut years off the payoff and save hundreds or thousands in interest. Try nudging the payment up to see it.
