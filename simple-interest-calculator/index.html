How this simple interest calculator works

Simple interest is the most straightforward way to charge or earn interest. It is calculated only on the original principal — never on interest that has already accrued — using the formula:

I = P · r · t

where I is the interest, P is the principal you start with, r is the annual interest rate written as a decimal (so 5% becomes 0.05), and t is the time in years. Your final total is simply P + I. Because the rate always applies to the same starting amount, the interest you earn is the same every year and the balance grows in a perfectly straight line.

Simple interest vs. compound interest

The key difference is what the interest is calculated on. With simple interest, every year you earn the same amount because it is always P · r. With compound interest, each period's interest is added to the balance, so the next period earns interest on a bigger number — your money snowballs.

Over one year the two are identical. Over many years the gap widens fast: at 5% on $10,000, simple interest pays a flat $500 a year, while compounding pays a little more each year as the balance climbs. If you are saving or investing for the long term, compounding works in your favour — see the difference on our compound interest calculator.

When you will run into simple interest

  • Short-term and fixed loans. Many auto loans, personal loans, and car-title loans quote simple interest on the principal.
  • Bonds and notes. A fixed coupon paid on the face value is simple interest — the interest does not get reinvested into the bond itself.
  • Quick estimates. Simple interest is a fast, conservative way to ballpark the cost of borrowing or the return on a short deposit before reaching for a compounding model.

Frequently asked questions

How is simple interest calculated?

Simple interest uses I = P · r · t, where P is the principal, r is the annual rate as a decimal, and t is the time in years. Your final total is P + I — the original amount plus the interest.

What is the difference between simple and compound interest?

Simple interest is charged only on the original principal, so it grows in a straight line. Compound interest is charged on the principal plus any interest already added, so it grows faster over time. For multi-year savings or debt, compound interest is almost always larger — compare on the compound interest calculator.

Can I use months instead of years?

Yes. Enter the time as a fraction of a year — 6 months is 0.5, 18 months is 1.5, and 90 days is roughly 0.25. The formula multiplies the annual rate by the time, so a partial year gives a proportional amount of interest.

Where is simple interest actually used?

Many short-term and fixed loans use simple interest: auto loans, some personal and car-title loans, short bridge loans, and bonds or notes that pay a fixed coupon. It is common any time interest does not get added back to the balance.
